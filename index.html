Germany (Deutschland) is a country in Central and Western Europe, covering an area of 137,874 sq mi. It borders Denmark to the North, Poland and the Czech Republic to the East, Austria and Switzerland to the South and France, Luxembourg, Belgium and Netharlands to the West.

Germany Natural Resources:

Fuel resources for Germany include coal, lignite, natural gas and uranium. The mineral resources for the country include iron ore, copper and nickel. Salt, potash, construction materials, timber and able land are also a few of the viable resources for Germany.

Germany Nautral Hazards:

One of the natural hazards for Germany is flooding.

Environmental issues:

Germany has a number of issues concerning the environment like air pollution, partly due to the emissions from coal-burning utilities and industries. Resulting from the sulfur dioxide emissions is acid rain, which is damaging the forests. There is pollution in the Baltic Sea from raw sewage, and in the rivers of eastern Germany from industrial effluents. There are still issues with the disposal of hazardous waste.

Things to know before travelling to Germany:

  • Cash is King:

    Very few places accept credit cards as payment. It is always helpful to have cash in hand, just in case you run into a place that is cash only. The stores won’t tell you in advance, they don’t even have signs at the entrance. You just need to know.


  • Respect the rules:

    Always wait for the traffic light to go green before crossing the road. Jaywalking is absolutely disapproved. If you’re planning to ride a bicycle (and you should) you always need to have a back and front light for safety reasons. Being caught in the dark or running red lights both result in hefty fines.


  • Shop before Sunday :

    Aside from a couple of shops, usually near the train station, most supermarkets are closed on a Sunday. In Germany, Sunday is meant for relaxing.


  • Punctuality is key:

    Punctuality is taken seriously in Germany, and if you’re meeting up with someone you should always be on time. Traffic is not an excuse.


  • Pay for AND validate your train tickets:

    There is no security guard or person checking tickets as you enter the train stations, but that is not a free pass to ride the underground, as ticket checkers (wearing plain clothes) are also riding the subway and could pounce at any moment, costing you at least a €60 fine. Also, don't forget to validate your ticket.


  • Eating out:

    No matter where you are in Germany, you’re bound to find a street with a place to eat. In smaller towns, you’ll probably only find sausage and meat on the menu, while bigger cities like Berlin have a vegetarian and vegan menu. If you’re eating on a budget, look out an ‘Imbiss’, a cheap snack shop that can be found on almost any busy street, train station, market and even parking lot.
